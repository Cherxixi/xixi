The Ancient Origins

Tea culture began in ancient Southwest China, where it was initially used as a medicinal beverage. Legend has it that the Chinese Emperor Shen Nong discovered tea in 2737 BC when leaves from a nearby bush fell into his boiling water. Over thousands of years, tea evolved from a medicine to a daily staple and a sophisticated art form, spreading across Asia and eventually the entire world via the Silk Road and maritime trade.

Popular Varieties

All "true" teas come from the Camellia sinensis plant. The difference lies in how the leaves are processed and oxidized.

Green Tea

Unoxidized leaves that are heated quickly to preserve their green color and fresh, grassy flavor. Rich in antioxidants.

Black Tea

Fully oxidized leaves that offer a bold, strong flavor and dark amber color. Popular in Western cultures (e.g., Earl Grey).

Oolong Tea

Partially oxidized, sitting between green and black tea. Known for complex, floral, and fruity aromatic profiles.

White Tea

The least processed tea, made from young buds and leaves. It has a delicate, naturally sweet taste.

Tea Ceremonies & Philosophy

Beyond being a drink, tea is a philosophy. In many cultures, the preparation of tea is a meditative ritual:

  • Chinese Gongfu Tea: Focuses on the skill (Gongfu) of brewing, emphasizing the aroma and multiple infusions of the leaves.
  • Japanese Chanoyu: A highly ritualized spiritual practice influenced by Zen Buddhism, centered on the preparation of Matcha.
  • British Afternoon Tea: A social tradition introduced in the 19th century, involving tea served with sandwiches and scones.

The core values often revolve around Harmony, Respect, Purity, and Tranquility.
